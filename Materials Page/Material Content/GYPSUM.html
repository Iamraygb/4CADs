GYPSUM

SUMMARY

Gypsum is a naturally occurring mineral composed of calcium sulfate dihydrate (CaSO₄·2H₂O). It is widely used in the construction industry to produce gypsum boards, plaster, and other building materials. Its ease of use, versatility, and fire-resistant properties make it a key material in modern construction.

Gypsum is commonly used in making drywall (gypsum board), which is extensively utilized for interior walls and ceilings. It is also used for plastering walls, creating decorative moldings, and manufacturing cement as a setting regulator. In agriculture, gypsum improves soil structure and fertility.

Gypsum is lightweight, easy to shape, and has excellent fire-resistant properties due to its high water content. It is also sound-insulating and provides a smooth surface for finishing. While it is non-combustible, it is vulnerable to water damage, which can compromise its strength and integrity.

Gypsum is abundant and can often be recycled from demolition waste, reducing the need for new extraction. Synthetic gypsum, a by-product of industrial processes like flue-gas desulfurization, further enhances sustainability by reducing waste. However, its environmental impact depends on the energy used in processing and transportation.

Major gypsum manufacturers include Saint-Gobain (Gyproc), USG Corporation, National Gypsum, Knauf, and Etex. These companies produce a variety of gypsum products, including standard and specialty boards, plasters, and additives for diverse construction and industrial applications.
